LESSON PLAN IN ARGUMENTATIVE TEXT

I. OBJECTIVES
A. CONTENT STANDARD The Learner understands the structural elements of an argumentative text.
B. PERFORMANCE STANDARD The Learner writes a clear argumentative text using correct structure.
C. LEARNING COMPETENCY At the end of the lesson, learners will be able to:
1. Cognitive Domain: a. Identify key structural elements of an argumentative text;
2. Psychomotor Domain: b. Organize an argumentative text following the correct structure: exposition, arguments, and restatement of position; and
3. Affective Domain: c. Display responsibility in constructing argumentative text.

ABSTRACTION
Our topic for today is about Identifying key structural elements.

I. Structural Framework of Argumentation
The primary objective of our lesson is to enable students to map the skeletal architecture of a persuasive piece. This involves identifying three core phases:

1. Exposition: Establishing the Foundation
The exposition is the critical opening phase where the author lays the groundwork for their entire argument. This involves several key actions:
  • Introduction of the Topic: The author must first engage the reader by presenting the subject matter. This can be done through various means, such as a compelling anecdote, a striking statistic, a rhetorical question, or a broad overview of the issue. The aim is to capture attention and signal the relevance of the topic.
  • Contextualization: Providing necessary background information is crucial. This helps the reader understand the broader landscape in which the argument is situated, including historical context, prevailing societal views, or the specific circumstances that necessitate the argument.
  • Authoritative Thesis Statement: This is the cornerstone of the exposition. The thesis is a clear, concise, and assertive declaration of the author's main position or claim. It acts as a roadmap for the rest of the argument, informing the reader precisely what the author intends to prove or persuade them of. An authoritative thesis is typically debatable and specific, setting a definitive scope for the ensuing discussion.
2. Arguments: The Pillars of Persuasion
This is the substantive core of the persuasive piece, where the author systematically builds their case. This section is characterized by the presentation and development of evidence and reasoning. We will meticulously categorize the types of support provided:
  • Empirical Data: This refers to evidence derived from observation and experimentation, such as statistics, scientific findings, and factual reports. It provides objective grounding for the claims.
  • Anecdotal Evidence: This involves using personal stories, experiences, or specific examples to illustrate a point. While often relatable and impactful, it's important to recognize that anecdotes can be subjective and may not represent a broader trend.
  • Ethical Appeals (Ethos): This involves leveraging the author's credibility, character, or moral standing to persuade the audience. It can also involve appealing to the audience's sense of morality or shared values.
  • Logical Reasoning: This is the process of using a rational, systematic series of steps based on sound judgment and facts to reach a conclusion. Common forms include deductive reasoning (moving from general principles to specific conclusions) and inductive reasoning (moving from specific observations to broader generalizations).
  • Counter-arguments and Rebuttals: A sophisticated argument often acknowledges opposing viewpoints. Identifying counter-arguments demonstrates a comprehensive understanding of the issue, and the subsequent rebuttal effectively dismantles these opposing claims, thereby strengthening the author's original position.
3. Restatement of Position: The Culmination and Call to Action
The conclusion is far more than a mere recapitulation of points. It serves as the final opportunity for the author to solidify their argument and leave a lasting impression on the reader. This phase involves:
  • Synthesis of Arguments: Instead of simply listing the points made, the author should synthesize them, demonstrating how each piece of evidence and line of reasoning has contributed to the overall thesis. This shows the interconnectedness of the argument's components.
  • Reinforcement of the Thesis: The original thesis is revisited, not necessarily in the exact same words, but with renewed emphasis, reflecting the persuasive journey the reader has just undertaken. The conclusion should leave no doubt about the author's stance.
  • Broader Implications or Call to Action: A powerful conclusion often extends beyond the immediate argument. It might discuss the wider significance of the issue, suggest future research directions, or, most importantly, issue a clear and compelling call to action. This prompts the reader to engage with the ideas presented, whether by adopting a new perspective, taking a specific action, or further investigating the topic.
